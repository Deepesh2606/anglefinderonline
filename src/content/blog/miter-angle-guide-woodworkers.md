Achieving seamless, gap-free joints is one of the most rewarding aspects of woodworking. Whether you are building a simple picture frame, a hexagonal shadow box, or installing crown molding around a dining room wall, success depends on cutting angles with absolute accuracy.

A mistake of just half a degree can compound over a multi-sided box and leave ugly, open joints. In this guide, we break down standard miter cuts, polygon framing formulas, and how to use our online woodworking calculators to get your saw angles right the first time.

## Standard Miter Joints vs. Polygon Frames

A **miter joint** is made by joining two boards cut at an angle. For a standard 90-degree corner (like a picture frame), each board is cut at 45 degrees.

If you are building a regular polygon box (where all sides are equal), the formula to find the miter cut angle is:

$$\text{Miter Saw Angle} = \frac{180^\circ}{\text{Number of Sides}}$$

Here is a quick reference table for common polygon frames:
- **Triangle (3 sides):** $60^\circ$ cut
- **Square (4 sides):** $45^\circ$ cut
- **Hexagon (6 sides):** $30^\circ$ cut
- **Octagon (8 sides):** $22.5^\circ$ cut

*Note: The saw miter scale starts at $0^\circ$ (which represents a straight $90^\circ$ crosscut). Therefore, a $45^\circ$ miter cut means you rotate the saw table $45^\circ$ away from center.*

## What is a Compound Miter Cut?

A **compound miter cut** involves adjusting both the **miter angle** (rotating the saw table) and the **bevel angle** (tilting the saw blade) at the same time. This is required when cutting boards that slope upward and outward, such as shadow boxes, sloped trays, or crown molding.

To calculate these settings, you need to know:
1. **The Wall Corner Angle:** Usually 90 degrees, but often varies in residential plasterboard corners (e.g. 89° or 91°).
2. **The Spring/Slope Angle:** The angle at which the molding sits relative to the wall (typically 38 degrees or 45 degrees for standard US moldings).

### Compound Formulas:

$$\text{Miter Setting} = \arctan\left(\frac{\sin(\text{Slope})}{\tan(\text{Wall}/2)}\right)$$

$$\text{Bevel Setting} = \arcsin\left(\cos(\text{Slope}) \times \sin(\text{Wall}/2)\right)$$
