For roofers, contractors, and DIY home renovators, understanding **roof pitch** is essential. The pitch of a roof determines the types of shingles or roofing materials you can install, impacts drainage, and dictates rafter lengths and pricing.

Traditionally, measuring a roof required climbing a ladder and using a mechanical pitch finder. However, safety standards and modern technology now allow you to compute pitch ratios accurately from the ground using photos.

---

## What is Roof Pitch?

In the United States, roof pitch represents the slope of a roof and is expressed as a ratio of **vertical rise** to **horizontal run** over a span of 12 inches. 

For example, a **"4:12 pitch"** (or simply "4 pitch") means the roof rises 4 inches vertically for every 12 inches of horizontal run.

### The Math: Pitch to Degree Conversion

To find the angle ($\theta$) in degrees from a pitch ratio:

$$\theta = \arctan\left(\frac{\text{Rise}}{\text{Run}}\right) \times \frac{180}{\pi}$$

Since the run is always 12 inches, for a 4:12 pitch:

$$\theta = \arctan\left(\frac{4}{12}\right) = \arctan(0.3333) \approx 18.43^\circ$$

To convert a measured angle back to rise in inches:

$$\text{Rise} = 12 \times \tan(\theta)$$

## Slope Classifications

Roofs are generally classified into three categories based on steepness:
1. **Low Slope (1:12 to 3:12):** Very flat. Requires specialized materials like rubber membranes (EPDM) or standing seam metal to prevent water ponding.
2. **Medium Slope (4:12 to 8:12):** The most common residential slopes. Suitable for standard asphalt shingles.
3. **Steep Slope (9:12 to 12:12+):** Very steep. Common in snowy climates to shed load. Requires safety harnesses for installers.
